About the Map

This world map presents data on air pollution indices as calculated by the Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation (IHME). IHME has developed its own metrics for assessing air quality, measuring air pollutant particles in parts per billion.

A higher index value indicates an increased risk of illness due to water contamination, compromised food supplies, and the spread of diseases.

How to Read the Map

To find out the specific air pollution levels in various locations, simply hover over the countries on the map that interest you.

Doing so will showcase the continent, sub region and the exact air pollutant in the air.

Asia has the highest average share of deaths due to air pollution, followed by Oceania and Africa. Europe has the lowest average death rate (IHME 2019) as of 2016.

What Does This Mean?

The data suggests that the share of deaths attributable to air pollution is decreasing globally over time, according to the IHME index. This could imply various factors, such as:

Improved air quality, enhanced healthcare systems, advances in medical treatments, and an overall shift in the leading causes of death.

Analysis of Countries with the Worst IHME Air Pollution Index

Measured in parts per billion

Orange indicates: They remain top 5 most pollutant. Red indicates: Country entered top 5 most pollutant. Blue Indicates: Country left top 5 most pollutant

North Korea and Solomon Islands remain in the top 5 worst both in 1990 and 2016 .

North Korea maintained its position as the most polluted country, however, its air pollution level declined from 17.19 to 16.51 over 36 years. Similarly,Solomon Islands experienced a decrease in pollution levels and moved up in ranking from 5th to 4th place.

On the other hand, new countries emerged in the 2016 data, notably Bangladesh, India, and Nepal. These countries were not present in the 1990 dataset. Conversely, countries like China, Cambodia, and Tajikistan, which were part of the 1990 list, are no longer in top 5 worst air polluted countries.

Overall, while some countries have managed to reduce their air pollution levels, new countries have appeared on the list, indicating shifts in the global landscape of air pollution.

How do we do better?

Figure 2.1: Cause of air polluntant in the air for the 7 most populated sub regions

As evident from Figure 2.1, a majority of air pollutant-related deaths and damages are due to household activities. This is a concerning trend that is closely followed by damages due to ambient particles and damaged ozone layers. The data suggests that immediate action is required to combat these household-generated pollutants. Failing to do so could lead to a worsening public health crisis and contribute significantly to climate change.

If we aim to significantly reduce these pollutants, several measures could be taken. For instance, the widespread use of clean cooking fuels and technologies could drastically minimize household air pollution. In addition, enforcing stricter emission standards for industries and vehicles can help reduce ambient particles. Conservation initiatives aimed at repairing the ozone layer could further diminish air pollution levels. Hence, a multi-pronged approach is essential for mitigating the adverse effects of air pollution.
